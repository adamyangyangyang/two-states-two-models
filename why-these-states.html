Chapter 02 · Framing

Why Texas and California.

Why are Texas and California a meaningful pair to compare, and why these districts within them?

Texas and California are a meaningful pair to compare because they are two of the largest and most influential public school systems in the United States, and both serve highly diverse student populations. At the same time, they embody different answers to a central question in American education: how much responsibility for financing and governing schooling should rest with the state, and how much should rest with local communities? Using them together makes it possible to compare similar educational needs under very different institutional structures.

A — Why these two states

It would be appropriate to compare Texas and California because they are similar in scale but different in design. Both educate a great numbers of students, both contain large urban districts alongside affluent suburbs and poorer rural communities, and both serve racially, linguistically, and economically diverse populations. That means the comparison is not simply between a large system and a small system, or between a homogeneous population and a diverse one. Instead, it is a comparison between two states facing many of the same demographic pressures but responding to them through different funding and governance models. (The demographic similarities and differences are explored in detail in Chapter 04.)

District-level demographic profiles drawn from NCES Education Demographic and Geographic Estimates (EDGE) ACS dashboards: LAUSD · HISD.

The most important difference between the two states is structural. California relies more heavily on state funding and state-level redistribution, while Texas relies more heavily on local property wealth and local tax capacity. The district-level finance data show this clearly: in 2024–25, LAUSD received 54% of its funding from the state and 26% from local sources, while HISD received only 8% from the state and 69% from local sources. Per-student revenue also differed sharply, with LAUSD at $28,702 and HISD at $15,124. That makes Texas and California especially useful for studying how finance rules shape educational opportunity rather than simply describing differences in student outcomes.

Source: National Center for Education Statistics, Common Core of Data district search tool, 2024–25 school year. District detail: LAUSD · HISD.

Comparing these two states also helps isolate the effect of state structure from the effect of student need. Both states educate large numbers of low-income students, English learners, and students of color, so differences in governance and finance cannot easily be dismissed as the result of serving completely different populations. In this way, Texas and California let us ask whether different institutional choices lead similar student needs to be met or to be met different ways.

B — Why LAUSD and HISD as urban cases

Los Angeles Unified School District and Houston Independent School District work well as representative urban cases because both are very large districts serving complex student populations. Both are major metropolitan districts rather than unusually small or atypical systems, and both sit inside states whose funding structures are central to this project. They therefore make the state comparison more concrete: instead of discussing California and Texas only at the abstract statewide level, LAUSD and HISD show how those systems operate on the ground.

They are also useful because they hold several important conditions roughly constant. Both are urban districts serving substantial Latino, low-income, and English-learner populations. That means the comparison is not between a wealthy suburb and a struggling city, but between two broadly comparable urban systems embedded in different state structures. Because of such similarities, comparing LAUSD and HISD makes it easier to see how differences in state finance and governance show through.

C — Why Highland Park appears later

Highland Park Independent School District appears later in the project for a different reason. It is not meant to represent the average Texas district. Instead, it functions as a within-Texas contrast that shows what happens when the same state rules operate on a very different local tax base. Highland Park (Dallas County) has a median housing value of $1,731,100, a poverty rate of 4.5%, and per-student funding of $16,387, which is higher than HISD’s $15,124, despite drawing 91% of its revenue from local sources. That makes Highland Park useful not as a “typical” district, but as evidence that local property wealth matters inside the Texas system itself.

Source: NCES Common Core of Data, 2024–25 school year. Highland Park ISD district detail.

D — A note on method

  • The main frame of this project is a state-level comparison between Texas and California.
  • LAUSD and HISD are used as representative large urban district cases within those systems.
  • Highland Park ISD is used as a within-Texas contrast, not as a representative Texas district.
  • Data years vary somewhat across sources and datasets and are noted.
  • Voting and political patterns and structures are used as contextual evidence, not as proof of causation.
