Chapter 05 · Finance & Community Wealth

How funding rules convert local wealth into school resources.

How do California and Texas school finance structures translate community wealth into school resources, and how does that shape what students get?

Both states pay for public education with a mix of federal, state, and local dollars, but they weigh those sources very differently. California relies on state redistribution to buffer schools from the inequalities built into local property wealth. Texas relies on local property taxes and treats districts more like fiscal units responsible for their own base.

A — The structures, side by side

Public K–12 schools in both California and Texas are funded by a combination of federal, state, and local revenues. The federal share is small in both places. The real difference between the two states is in how the remaining majority of funding gets divided between state and local sources, and in what logic decides who pays for what.

California distributes most K–12 funding through the Local Control Funding Formula (LCFF), enacted in 2013. LCFF sets a base grant per student and then layers additional “supplemental” and “concentration” grants on top for districts that enroll English learners, low-income students, and foster youth. Crucially, LCFF is funded primarily out of state revenue, a legacy of Proposition 13, which in 1978 capped local property tax rates and effectively pushed school funding responsibility up to Sacramento. The state is the default payer; local districts fill in relatively little on top.

Texas runs the Foundation School Program (FSP), a system built around an opposite default. The state sets a per-pupil funding target, and each district’s local property tax is expected to raise most of that target first, with the state making up what property wealth can’t reach. Districts whose property wealth exceeds the target are subject to recapture, informally known as “Robin Hood,” which returns excess revenue to the state to redistribute. In theory, the FSP floor plus recapture is equalizing. In practice, the local property tax does most of the heavy lifting, and districts with weaker property wealth have to work harder just to reach the floor.

Both systems can be described as “equalizing.” But one equalizes by making the state the primary funder and tuning allocations to student need, and the other equalizes by making the local tax base the primary funder and redistributing excess revenue from wealthier districts. Those are very different defaults, and they produce very different funding floors and ceilings.

B — Two urban districts, almost inverted

The clearest way to see the difference is to hold the student population structure roughly constant and compare two large urban districts: the Los Angeles Unified School District (LAUSD) and the Houston Independent School District (HISD). Both enroll majority-Latino student populations. Both educate large numbers of English learners. Both serve high concentrations of low-income students. Their funding components from state and local sources, however, are almost mirror images.

LAUSD pulls a larger share of its budget from state sources precisely because California’s post–Prop 13 architecture makes the state the default payer. HISD pulls most of its budget from local property taxes because Texas’s architecture expects it to. The per-student revenue gap of roughly $13,500 is not explained by any single factor: cost of living, formula weighting, program mix, and enrollment decline all play roles. But it is far too large to dismiss as a rounding difference, and it is consistent with a broader pattern. California districts that serve high-need students generally have more total revenue per pupil than comparable Texas districts.

C — Why this gap isn’t incidental

Why does Texas’s local-tax-heavy structure matter so much? Because the local tax base is itself unequal. Property values vary enormously across districts, and a school finance system that asks local property wealth to do most of the work will, almost by definition, convert differences in property wealth into differences in school resources. Districts with weaker tax bases have less to work with. Districts with stronger tax bases have more. That isn’t a bug of the Texas system, but it is close to the system’s logic, modified only at the top by recapture.

California’s architecture does not eliminate the link between local wealth and school resources, but it muffles it. Because state dollars make up the larger share of most California district budgets, differences in local property wealth translate less directly into differences in what schools can spend. A low-wealth district in California is cushioned by a formula that is indifferent to the local tax base; a low-wealth district in Texas is cushioned only by whatever top-up the FSP delivers after the local tax is collected.

Housing values, household income, and poverty rates are so important in these two systems and are not just economic background. They are part of the funding mechanism itself. In HISD, where property wealth is stretched across a large and economically uneven city, the tax base cannot fully meet the need. In LAUSD, where property values are high and where Proposition 13 has already decoupled much of the funding from those values, the state is doing more of the buffering.

D — Same state, different tax base: Highland Park

The Texas structure is easiest to see not by comparing Texas to California, but by comparing Texas districts to each other. Highland Park Independent School District is a small, affluent district in the Dallas metro. It sits in one of the wealthiest ZIP codes in the country. Its students are overwhelmingly white and high-income. Its local property tax base, on a per-student basis, is enormous.

Because Texas leans on local property wealth, Highland Park has been able to raise substantial per-pupil revenue from its own tax base, even after paying recapture back to the state. Because of having more funding, the schools can have newer facilities, smaller class sizes, and richer course offerings than schools in HISD just a few hours away. Recapture blunts the top of the distribution, but it does not flatten it.

The comparison matters because Highland Park and HISD operate under exactly the same Texas rules. The difference between them is not policy. It is tax base. That is precisely the kind of within-state inequality that a state-equalized system like California’s is designed to weaken, and it is the kind of inequality that a locally-funded system is, by construction, less equipped to address.

The Takeaway
School finance is not just about how much money states spend. It is about how funding rules convert differences in local wealth into differences in school resources.

Differences in resources do not automatically produce differences in outcomes. Money matters, but it matters through teachers hired, programs offered, counselors funded, and buildings maintained, and those translations can be stronger or weaker depending on governance and implementation.
