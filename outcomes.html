Chapter 06 · Outcomes & Inequality

What the systems actually produce.

What outcomes do these systems produce — and what patterns of opportunity and inequality appear in them?

This page is deliberately not a scoreboard. Drawing on the Stanford Education Data Archive (SEDA), it compares two things: what students score on average, and how much they actually improve each year in school. The picture is more complicated than the finance story alone would predict. Texas students score closer to the national average than California students do, even though California spends more per pupil. But California’s students learn slightly more per grade — a better proxy for what schools contribute — and the more important pattern in both states is that within-state inequality by race, income, and language is large.

A — A note on how to read these numbers

The Educational Opportunity Project at Stanford uses standardized test data to express outcomes in units of grade levels. A score of “0” represents the national average; a score of “−0.63 grade levels” means a state’s students are testing as if they were about two-thirds of a school year behind the national average. Two measures matter most here. Average test scores capture the total set of educational opportunities students experience — at home, in their neighborhoods, and in school combined. Learning rates capture how much students improve per year while they are in school, and are generally considered a better proxy for school quality alone. Thinking about both together is the whole point.

B — The surprising pattern in averages

On average test scores, Texas looks better than California. Texas students score about 0.12 grade levels below the national average; California students score 0.63 grade levels below. That gap is not trivial — it is roughly half a school year. And it runs in the direction opposite to the funding story in the previous chapter: the state that spends more per pupil has lower average test scores.

Before interpreting that as an indictment of California, it is worth pausing on what average scores actually measure. SEDA makes the point directly: average scores reflect everything shaping a student’s test performance, including early childhood experiences, neighborhood resources, housing stability, and family income. California’s cost of living, its very large foreign-born and English-learner population, and its acute housing crisis all depress the conditions that feed into average test scores, even when schools themselves are doing substantial work. That does not excuse the scores. But it does mean average scores are a poor measure of schools’ contribution in isolation.

C — Learning rates: what schools actually add

When the question shifts from “how are students doing” to “how much are they learning each year while they are in school,” the ranking flips. California students learn about 4% more per grade than the U.S. average; Texas students, about 1% more. Both states are above the national mean on this measure, but California is further above. That is consistent with the argument that California’s better-resourced schools are doing more per year of schooling than Texas’s, even if their students arrive at school less prepared on average.

Put differently: California schools are lifting kids a bit faster; California students simply have farther to climb. That is not the story anyone tells when they stop at average scores.

D — Gaps within states

The most important pattern on this page is not the state-to-state comparison at all. It is the size of the gaps within each state, because those gaps are the real test of whether a finance and governance system is reaching the students it claims to prioritize.

The gaps are substantial in both states. But California’s are actually larger than Texas’s on most comparisons. The white/Black gap in average test scores is 2.80 grade levels in California and 2.09 in Texas. The white/Hispanic gap is 2.27 in California and 1.62 in Texas. The non-poor/poor gap — the cleanest indicator of how well a state is serving its low-income students — is 2.75 in California and 2.12 in Texas. Those are differences of roughly half a grade level to two-thirds of a grade level, and they go in the same direction across every major subgroup comparison.

That finding complicates the tidy version of the thesis. A state that has done more to equalize per-pupil funding should, in principle, show smaller within-state gaps, not larger ones. Several things are probably going on. California’s Latino and Black student populations include larger shares of recent immigrants, English learners, and students in extremely high-cost housing markets — conditions that depress scores independently of school quality. California’s residential segregation, particularly in coastal metros, may also concentrate need in ways that undermine equalization. And most importantly, equalized dollars do not automatically translate into equalized services: LCFF’s supplemental and concentration grants are supposed to follow high-need students, but whether those dollars reach the classrooms and programs they were weighted for is a real, documented question in the California research literature.

E — Trends over time

Trajectories add a final piece of the picture. Between 2009 and 2018, California’s average test scores improved by about 0.04 grade levels per year — a slow but real rise in overall educational opportunity. Texas’s average scores fell slightly over the same period, by about 0.01 grade levels per year. Learning rate trends tell a quieter version of the same story: California’s learning rates held roughly flat; Texas’s learning rates declined by about 0.01 grade levels per year. Neither state is dramatically improving or collapsing, but the direction is upward in California and flat-to-downward in Texas. That is consistent with the hypothesis that sustained investment and formula-based equalization do produce slow gains over time, even when they do not show up in a single-year snapshot.

F — What the outcomes suggest, honestly

Taken together, the SEDA data support a modest version of the thesis rather than a triumphant one. California’s higher funding appears to contribute to somewhat better learning rates and a gentle upward trend in scores, which is what you would hope to see from a state that spends more per student. But California’s within-state gaps are larger than Texas’s, which suggests that equalized state funding alone does not close opportunity gaps for high-need students. Money matters, but its effects depend on how dollars reach classrooms, how segregated those classrooms are, and how much out-of-school need accumulates before students ever arrive. Texas’s lower-resource system, meanwhile, produces smaller gaps partly because it produces less growth for everyone. Smaller gaps are not always evidence of a more equitable system; sometimes they are evidence of a flatter ceiling.

Bridge
Finance structures do not determine outcomes by themselves, but they shape the conditions under which schools try to improve them.
