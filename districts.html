Chapter 07 · The District Lens

Three districts, two states, one argument.

What do LAUSD, HISD, and Highland Park show us about how state systems operate on the ground?

Abstractions about “state structure” become concrete when we look at individual districts. LAUSD shows California’s state-buffered model in action: a high-need urban district with relatively high per-student revenue because the state plays a major role in funding. HISD shows the limits of Texas’s local-tax-heavy model in a high-need urban district. Highland Park ISD shows what the same Texas rules look like when the local tax base is wealthy. Bringing in district-level outcomes rather than finance alone reveals that funding structures shape capacity, but capacity is only one of the forces that determines what students actually learn.

LAUSD illustrates California’s state-buffered funding model. Even though LAUSD serves a large, urban, high-need student population, it receives a majority of its revenue from the state rather than depending mainly on local property taxes. This matters because California’s funding structure partially separates school resources from local property wealth, giving LAUSD a much higher per-student revenue capacity than HISD despite both districts serving large urban populations.

At the same time, LAUSD is not a low-need district. Its community is majority Hispanic, with roughly one in five families living below the federal poverty line, and a clear majority of school-age children speak a language other than English at home. This means LAUSD’s relatively high per-student revenue should not be read simply as “extra money,” but as funding capacity needed to serve a large and complex urban student body.

The outcome data complicate this story rather than confirming it. LAUSD’s average test scores sit 1.44 grade levels below the national average, and its learning rates run roughly 7% below the U.S. mean. Both numbers fall below California’s statewide figures from the previous chapter. The trends, however, point in a more hopeful direction: average test scores rose by about 0.05 grade levels per year between 2009 and 2018, and learning rates inched up by about 0.01 per year over the same period. Read together, these numbers suggest that California’s state-buffered funding gives LAUSD the capacity to support a deeply high-need population and to produce gradual gains in both opportunity and school performance, but capacity alone has not been enough to lift average performance to the national mean.

HISD illustrates a different model. Like LAUSD, it is a major urban district with high student need, but unlike LAUSD, it receives only a small share of its revenue from the state and depends heavily on local revenue. Because local funding in Texas is closely tied to property-tax capacity, HISD’s lower housing values and higher poverty context help explain why its per-student revenue is much lower than LAUSD’s.

This makes HISD especially important for the project’s argument. It shows that student need and funding capacity do not automatically move together. HISD serves a student population with substantial economic and language-support needs, yet its local-tax-heavy funding structure produces much less per-student revenue than LAUSD’s more state-supported model.

The outcome picture for HISD is more mixed than the funding picture suggests. Average test scores fall 0.58 grade levels below the national average, but learning rates land roughly at the U.S. mean, and SEDA reports them about 3% above what would be expected from districts with similar socioeconomic profiles. Both the test-score trend and the learning-rate trend across 2009–2018 are essentially flat. The story here is not one of failing schools but of schools producing roughly average growth despite below-average resources, in a district where out-of-school need is high and the local tax base cannot fully meet it.

Highland Park ISD is not used here as a representative Texas district. Instead, it is a within-Texas contrast that shows what happens when the same Texas funding rules operate in a much wealthier community. Compared with HISD, Highland Park has far higher local property wealth, much lower family poverty, and a community where the overwhelming majority of school-age children speak English at home.

The funding pattern reflects that difference. Highland Park receives 91% of its revenue from local sources and only 6% from the state, while still reporting higher per-student revenue than HISD. It also operates as a recapture district, meaning it collects a large amount of local property-tax revenue and sends part of it back to the state for redistribution. This makes Highland Park a useful example of how local wealth remains powerful inside the Texas system, even with recapture.

Highland Park’s outcomes look exceptional in absolute terms. Average test scores sit 2.81 grade levels above the national average, the strongest of the three districts, and learning rates run 6% above the U.S. mean. But the comparison that matters most is to similar-SES districts elsewhere. On that benchmark, SEDA reports Highland Park scoring about 0.48 grade levels lower than peer high-SES districts, with learning rates 13% lower. Both trends across 2009–2018 also drift downward: average test scores fall by about 0.06 grade levels per year and learning rates by about 0.03. High raw scores reflect community advantage at least as much as school value-added. The district is wealthy in inputs and in outputs both, but it is not over-performing relative to its socioeconomic context.

What these three districts show together

Taken together, these three districts help isolate the mechanism behind the broader Texas–California comparison. The LAUSD–HISD contrast shows how two large urban districts with substantial student need can end up with very different resource levels because they sit inside different state finance systems. LAUSD is embedded in a California system where the state plays a much larger role in school revenue. HISD is embedded in a Texas system where local property-tax revenue plays a much larger role.

The HISD–Highland Park contrast then shows that the issue is not only California versus Texas. It is also about what happens within Texas when the same state rules meet very different local economic conditions. HISD’s lower home values and higher poverty weaken its local revenue capacity, while Highland Park’s high property wealth allows it to generate stronger local resources. In that sense, Highland Park helps prove the argument more clearly: Texas’s funding structure does not affect all districts equally because local tax bases are unequal.

Together, the three cases show that school funding differences are shaped by the interaction between state policy design and local economic context. California’s model does more to buffer local inequality through state funding, while Texas’s model leaves more room for local property wealth to shape district resources. The result is that students’ educational resource capacity is not determined only by their needs, but also by the wealth and funding rules of the communities in which they attend school.

Adding outcomes to the picture makes the comparison more honest, not simpler. LAUSD has by far the highest per-student revenue of the three, but its average test scores remain well below the national mean. Its trajectory across the decade, however, is upward in both averages and learning rates. HISD has the lowest revenue and below-average scores, yet its learning rates are roughly average, slightly above what its socioeconomic profile would predict, and both of its trends are essentially flat. Highland Park has the strongest absolute scores but underperforms similar-SES districts, and both its score trend and its learning-rate trend drift gently downward. None of the three districts maps cleanly onto a “more money equals better outcomes” story.

What the three districts do show is that finance structures shape capacity, and capacity sets the conditions under which schools try to educate students. California’s model gives LAUSD the fiscal room to serve a high-need population and to generate gradual improvement over time. Texas’s model leaves HISD doing roughly average academic work with below-average resources, and lets Highland Park’s local advantage compound into both stronger funding and stronger absolute outcomes, even when the school value-added story turns out to be more modest. Money matters, but it matters through the conditions it creates, not as a direct cause of test scores.

Note · Finance figures here come from NCES district profiles for 2024–25, while the outcome figures come from the Stanford Education Data Archive, which covers 2009–2019. The two windows do not overlap. The working assumption in the comparison above is that the relative ordering of the three districts on per-student revenue has been broadly stable across the past two decades: that LAUSD has consistently sat well above HISD, and that Highland Park has consistently sat above HISD, throughout the SEDA period. Even under that assumption, the comparison should still be read as structural and descriptive rather than causal. It describes the kind of system each district sits inside, not a direct effect of recent funding levels on these particular test-score numbers.

Sidebar · Political context

Voting patterns are context, not explanation.

In the voting data collected earlier, LAUSD and HISD both leaned strongly Democratic in the selected urban precincts, even though their school finance systems differ. That suggests urban political context may be a confounding variable: large city districts may share voting patterns even when they operate under different state funding structures.

Highland Park differs politically and economically, but for this project, the cleaner explanatory variable is the tax base, not voting alone. Political geography is worth noting, but it is not the mechanism.
