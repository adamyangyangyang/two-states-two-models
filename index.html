Two States, Two Models

Local Control, State Responsibility, and Educational Opportunity in Texas and California

The Question

How do California and Texas organize responsibility for public education — and what do those choices produce?

The short version

Both states educate roughly six million public school students. Both rely on a mix of state, local, and federal dollars. But where the weight of responsibility sits — and how that weight is distributed — is almost inverted between them. This project traces what follows from that difference.

Thesis

California treats public education as a statewide obligation, using state revenue and a need-weighted formula to buffer schools from the inequalities built into local property wealth. Texas treats public education as a largely local responsibility, asking districts to fund their own base from property taxes while the state fills gaps. The structures equalize differently — and so do the schools.
