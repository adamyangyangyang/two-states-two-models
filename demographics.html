Chapter 04 · Demographics & Enrollment

Who these school systems serve.

Who do California and Texas public schools serve — and how is that changing?

California and Texas educate the two largest public student populations in the United States, and both now serve majority-minority populations with large Latino enrollments. But the two states are pulling apart demographically. California is older, wealthier on paper, and losing residents; Texas is younger, growing rapidly, and adding households at one of the highest rates in the country. Those demographic trajectories shape the budgets, housing markets, and tax bases that fund the schools.

A — State population context

California had an estimated population of 39.4 million in July 2024; Texas, 31.3 million. Both rank in the top two nationally. But the trajectories are diverging sharply. California’s population has declined by roughly 0.3% since April 2020, while Texas’s has grown by 7.3% over the same period. Texas is, in effect, adding the equivalent of a mid-sized state every few years, while California is losing residents to other states and seeing its enrollment base shrink. For schools, that means two very different pressures: Texas districts face growth pressure, building costs, and teacher-hiring pressure; California districts face enrollment decline and the budget consequences that follow when funding is tied to average daily attendance.

Texas is also a younger state. About 24.5% of Texans are under 18, compared with 21.3% of Californians; Texas has proportionally more school-age children relative to its total population. California, by contrast, has a larger share of residents aged 65 and over (16.5% vs 13.9%), which affects both tax bases and political priorities.

B — Racial and linguistic diversity

Both states are majority-minority. The share of residents identifying as white alone and not Hispanic is 33.6% in California and 38.7% in Texas. The two states also have almost exactly the same Hispanic population share — 40.8% in California and 40.3% in Texas — which is one of the most important reasons these two systems are comparable on student need. Where the states differ is in the rest of the composition. California’s population is 17.0% Asian alone, Texas’s only 6.4%. Texas’s population is 13.7% Black alone, more than twice California’s 6.4%. Both realities have direct schooling consequences: California’s larger Asian population includes substantial recent-immigrant communities with specific English-learner needs; Texas’s larger Black population is concentrated disproportionately in urban districts like HISD and Dallas ISD.

Language diversity amplifies the story. In California, 44.4% of residents who are older than 5 speak a language other than English at home compared to 35.1% in Texas. California is also more foreign-born: 27.0% of California residents were born outside the United States, compared with 17.6% of Texas residents. Both figures are well above the national average, but California’s linguistic complexity is larger. That translates directly into schooling costs such as bilingual staffing, translation services, English-learner programming, and outreach to immigrant families.

C — Economic foundation and the tax base

The demographic factors that matter most for the finance chapter ahead are the economic factors, and here the two states look very different. Compared to Texas, California has higher median household income ($99,122 vs $78,476) but also a lower poverty rate (11.8% vs 13.4%) and a much smaller share of uninsured residents under 65 (6.9% vs 19.2%). California residents are somewhat more weathy from the income perspective.

However, the numbers in housing are striking. The median value of owner-occupied homes is $734,700 in California and $283,800 in Texas, which is a ratio of roughly 2.6 to 1. That single fact is central to understanding why each state’s finance system is built the way it is. California’s housing values are so high that if school funding were driven by uncapped local property taxes, wealthy coastal districts would dwarf everyone else; Proposition 13’s cap was a direct response to that pressure. Texas’s lower housing values spread over a larger and faster-growing population mean the local property tax base is real but thinner per student, which is why a district like HISD, with weaker tax capacity per student, struggles to reach a good amount of funding even when it taxes at high rates.

D — Why all of this matters for schools

Demographics are not just background for this project; they are part of the funding mechanism itself. Texas’s faster population growth and younger age structure mean its schools face continuing enrollment pressure, which creates demand for new facilities, more teachers, and expanded transportation. California’s enrollment is shrinking, and because school funding is tied closely to student attendance, districts can lose revenue as they serve fewer students even if the per-student funding rate stays constant. Texas’s higher poverty rate and larger uninsured population also mean that schools often face greater student needs tied to health, family instability, and economic hardship, requiring more spending on support services outside the core classroom. California’s much greater linguistic diversity means English-learner programming is a larger and more unavoidable part of district budgets. Finally, California’s extremely high housing values could have produced enormous local school revenues under a pure property-tax system, but Proposition 13 limits how much local property-tax revenue can grow, which is why state funding plays such a large role in California school finance.

These are interconnected facts and feed directly into the next chapter, which is about the finance structures that convert all of this demographic reality into dollars and, eventually, into classrooms.
