Chapter 08 · Literature Review

What the research actually says.

What does existing research say about the relationship between school finance, governance, and educational opportunity?

The literature on education in California and Texas suggests that differences in school outcomes are best understood not simply as differences in student effort or district quality, but as the product of governance structures, finance systems, and broader social conditions. Across the readings reviewed here, a common theme emerges: public education is shaped by institutional choices about who holds responsibility for funding, how resources are distributed, and what counts as an adequate or equitable education. In that sense, Texas and California are useful not because one is simply “better,” but because they represent different models of how a state can organize educational opportunity.

A — Constitutional obligation and legal structure

One strand of the literature focuses on constitutional obligation and legal structure. Gordon’s (2016) analysis of California adequacy doctrine shows that the state’s courts have moved beyond a narrow view of education as mere access to schooling and toward a broader understanding of education as a meaningful constitutional right, shaped by cases such as Serrano, Hartzell, and Butt. That line of reasoning pushes California toward a stronger state obligation to equalize opportunity. By contrast, Lemke, Jackson, and Lehr’s (2014) overview of Texas school-finance litigation shows that Texas law has been dominated by the tension between the constitutional requirement for an “efficient system of public free schools” and the political commitment to local control. After Rodriguez foreclosed a federal equal-protection path, Texas litigation such as Edgewood and West Orange–Cove forced the state to reduce the most extreme property-wealth disparities, but without abandoning the local property-tax foundation of the system. Taken together, these readings suggest that the two states differ not only in policy design, but in the legal language through which educational inequality is recognized and addressed.

B — Governance and finance architecture

A second strand of the literature examines governance and finance architecture. Myung, Hough, and Marsh (2025) argue that California has moved over time away from heavy local-property-tax dependence and toward a more state-shaped system, especially after Serrano, Proposition 13, and the Local Control Funding Formula. Yet they also emphasize that California’s governance system remains fragmented, with authority spread across the state board, the superintendent, the department of education, county offices, districts, and charter authorizers. In other words, California’s model appears more equity-oriented in funding, but not necessarily simple or coherent in administration. The Texas litigation and finance materials present a contrasting model: Texas retains a stronger role for local taxation and local district identity, while relying on the Foundation School Program and recapture to partially offset the inequities created by uneven property wealth. This literature therefore frames Texas and California as different institutional answers to the same problem: how to balance state responsibility, local autonomy, and fiscal equity.

C — Does money matter?

A third body of research addresses the long-debated question of whether money matters. Handel and Hanushek’s (2023) review of U.S. school finance cautions against simplistic claims: the effects of spending on outcomes vary across contexts, and the central issue is not just how much money districts receive, but how resources are used and whether finance systems are aligned with student need. At the same time, Kreisman and Steinberg (2019) provide important causal evidence that additional funding can improve outcomes. Their study of Texas’s small district adjustment finds that increased foundation funding raised reading and math achievement and also improved longer-run indicators such as dropout rates, graduation, and college enrollment, with especially strong effects in poorer districts. Read together, these sources support a nuanced conclusion: money does matter, but it matters most when it is sustained, targeted, and delivered through institutions capable of translating resources into learning opportunities. This is especially relevant for a Texas–California comparison, because it suggests that finance structure is not merely a background condition — it is one mechanism through which states shape opportunity.

D — Gaps that predate schooling

Reardon and colleagues’ (2018) portrait of California educational outcomes adds another important layer by showing that opportunity gaps often predate formal schooling and persist through it. The report emphasizes that California has made progress in some areas, yet still performs unevenly across income groups, racial and ethnic groups, English learners, and geographic settings. It also suggests that many achievement gaps emerge before kindergarten, meaning that K–12 systems inherit inequalities that they did not create. This is an important corrective to overly school-centered explanations of educational inequality: schools matter, but they operate within broader structures of poverty, housing, health, and family resources. For this project, that means differences between Texas and California should not be interpreted only through test scores or per-pupil funding, but through how each system responds to high-need student populations and how effectively it mitigates inequalities that begin outside the classroom.

E — Inequality inside the schoolhouse

Antonovics, Black, Cullen, and Meiselman (2023) further complicate the picture by showing that inequality is produced inside schools as well as between districts. Their study of Texas ability tracking finds that sorting by prior achievement is shaped more strongly by classroom assignment than by school- or district-level sorting, suggesting that educational opportunity is not distributed evenly even after students enter the same district or the same school. They also find that tracking tends to benefit higher-achieving students more than lower-achieving students. This matters for the larger project because it shows that finance reform alone does not exhaust the problem of inequality. Even when funding levels improve, organizational practices such as tracking can continue to stratify opportunity within districts. The literature, in other words, points to a layered model of inequality: state policy structures resource differences across districts, while school-level practices can reproduce those differences within them.

Synthesis
California and Texas differ not just in spending levels, but in how they define the state’s educational duty, how they balance local control against equity, and how they channel community wealth into school resources.

The strongest conclusion across these readings is not that one variable alone explains educational opportunity, but that law, governance, finance, and school organization interact. For this project, that means the Texas–California comparison should be framed not as a simple contest of outcomes, but as a comparison of two institutional models for distributing educational opportunity under conditions of racial, economic, and geographic inequality.
